General & About Us

What does a Certified Aging In Place Specialist (CAPS) do?
A CAPS professional assesses your home for safety risks and recommends modifications — grab bars, walk-in showers, ramps, ceiling lifts, stairlifts — that let you live independently as you age. NW Homecare Medical holds all three CAPS certifications (I, II, and III) from the National Association of Home Builders. This means we've completed advanced training in home accessibility design, common remodeling solutions, and the business management needed to deliver safe, code-compliant installations.

We serve the greater Portland, Oregon metro area including Beaverton, Tigard, Lake Oswego, Gresham, Hillsboro, West Linn, Oregon City, Milwaukie, Tualatin, Sherwood, and surrounding communities throughout the Willamette Valley. If you're outside this area, contact us — we may still be able to help depending on the project scope.
Is NW Homecare Medical licensed and insured?
Yes. We are an Oregon Construction Contractors Board licensed and bonded contractor, CCB #247114. We carry full general liability insurance on every project. You can verify our license status on the Oregon CCB website.

What brands do you install?

This means we have factory training, access to full product lines, and every installation is backed by manufacturer warranties.
Do you offer free estimates?
Yes. We provide free in-home assessments where we evaluate your space, discuss your mobility needs, and deliver a written estimate with product options and pricing. There's no pressure and no obligation.

Process & Scheduling

What happens during a home assessment?
Matt visits your home and walks through the space with you (or your family member / caregiver). He evaluates room-by-room for fall hazards, accessibility barriers, and future mobility needs. You'll discuss which modifications would make the biggest impact, see product options, and receive a written plan with pricing — usually within a few days of the visit.
How long does installation take?
Most single-product installations are completed in one day:
  • Stairlift: 2–4 hours
  • Ceiling lift (single room): 4–6 hours
  • Wheelchair ramp: 4–8 hours depending on length
  • Bathroom remodel: 1–3 days depending on complexity
We'll provide a clear timeline during your assessment so there are no surprises.
Do I need to be home during installation?
Yes, we ask that you or a family member be present. After installation, we provide hands-on training for the individual and any caregivers on proper use, safety features, and basic maintenance. This training is included at no extra cost.
How far out are you typically booked?
Lead times vary by season and product availability. For grab bars and minor modifications, we can often schedule within 1–2 weeks. Bathroom remodels and ceiling lift systems may require 2–4 weeks for product ordering and scheduling. We'll give you a realistic timeline at your assessment.
Do you handle building permits?
Yes. When permits are required, we research the requirements for your specific municipality, prepare the application, and coordinate inspections. Many modular ramp and stairlift installations are exempt from permits, but we verify this on a case-by-case basis to ensure full code compliance.
What if I need modifications urgently — for example, after a hospital discharge?
We understand that hospital discharges can happen quickly. Contact us as early as possible and let us know the timeline — we'll do everything we can to prioritize urgent installations. Grab bars, threshold ramps, and some stairlift models can often be installed within days.

Cost & Financing

How much do aging in place modifications cost?
Costs vary by project type and scope:
  • Grab bars: $200–$400 per installation
  • Wheelchair ramp: $2,000–$8,000+ depending on length and configuration
  • Stairlift: $3,000–$6,000 including installation
  • Ceiling lift (single room): $3,000–$6,000
  • Ceiling lift (multi-room): $6,000–$12,000+
  • Accessible bathroom remodel: $5,000–$15,000+
Every project is unique. We provide free assessments with detailed written estimates so you know exactly what to expect.
Does Medicare cover aging in place home modifications?
Medicare generally does not cover structural home modifications like ramps, stairlifts, or bathroom remodels. However, certain items — particularly ceiling lift systems — may qualify as Durable Medical Equipment (DME) under Medicare Part B if prescribed by a physician. Coverage requires meeting specific medical necessity criteria and using a Medicare-approved supplier.
What about Medicaid, VA benefits, or other programs?
Several programs may help fund modifications:
  • Oregon Medicaid waivers: The Oregon Health Plan offers home modification benefits through certain waiver programs for eligible individuals.
  • VA Aid & Attendance: Veterans and surviving spouses may qualify for benefits that can be applied toward home accessibility improvements.
  • Long-term care insurance: Many policies cover home modifications as an alternative to facility care.
  • Area Agencies on Aging: Local agencies sometimes offer grants or low-interest loans for accessibility modifications.
We can help you understand which programs may apply to your situation.
Do you offer financing or payment plans?
We can discuss payment arrangements on a case-by-case basis. Contact us during your assessment and we'll work with you to find a solution that fits your budget.
Are aging in place modifications tax-deductible?
Home modifications that are medically necessary may qualify as deductible medical expenses on your federal tax return (to the extent they exceed the adjusted gross income threshold). Consult your tax advisor for guidance specific to your situation. We can provide detailed invoices for your records.

Bathroom Remodels

What is a barrier-free shower?
A barrier-free (or zero-threshold) shower has no step or curb to step over. This eliminates the #1 bathroom fall hazard and allows wheelchair or walker access directly into the shower area. The floor is gently sloped toward the drain so water stays contained without needing a raised threshold.
Can you remodel my existing bathroom without a full gut renovation?
In most cases, yes. Best Bath's multi-piece prefabricated shower systems are specifically designed to retrofit into existing bathroom spaces. We can often remove your old tub or shower and install a new accessible system within the same footprint — without tearing down walls or re-plumbing the entire room.
What is Best Bath's warranty?
Best Bath offers a 30-year warranty on their composite wall construction. Their gelcoat surfaces, hardware, and fixtures carry separate warranty terms. As a certified dealer, NW Homecare Medical ensures your installation meets all manufacturer specifications so your warranty is fully valid.
Do the shower walls look like tile?
Yes. Best Bath's Crestline luxury wall panels replicate the look of tile with realistic textures and patterns — but they're completely grout-free. This eliminates the mold, mildew, and maintenance headaches that come with traditional tile, while giving you a high-end aesthetic.
Can you add grab bars to an existing bathroom without a full remodel?
Absolutely. Grab bar installation is one of our most common standalone services. We install ADA-compliant stainless steel grab bars anchored to wall studs (or with specialized blocking) in showers, next to toilets, and anywhere else they're needed. This is often the most impactful safety improvement you can make for a few hundred dollars.

Ceiling Track Lifts

What is a ceiling track lift?
A ceiling track lift is a motorized hoist unit that rides along a rail system mounted to your ceiling. It uses a sling to safely lift and transfer a person between locations — bed to wheelchair, wheelchair to toilet, room to room — completely eliminating manual lifting by caregivers. This dramatically reduces injury risk for both the individual and the caregiver.
Can ceiling lifts go from room to room?
Yes. Molift rail systems support straight runs and curved sections (30°, 45°, 60°, and 90° turns), allowing transfers through doorways and between multiple rooms on a single continuous track. We design the rail path during your assessment based on which rooms the individual needs to access.
How much weight can a Molift ceiling lift support?
The Molift Air 200 supports up to 451 lbs (205 kg) and the Molift Air 300 supports up to 660 lbs (300 kg). We'll recommend the right model based on the individual's needs and any anticipated future requirements.
Will the rail system damage my ceiling?
No. The rail system mounts securely to ceiling joists or structural beams using engineered brackets. Once installed, it looks like a clean, low-profile track. If the system is ever removed, the mounting points leave only small holes that are easily patched and painted.
How does the ceiling lift charge?
Molift systems feature In-Rail Charging (IRC) — the hoist unit charges automatically while docked at a designated point on the rail. No separate charging station needed, no cables to manage. Just park the hoist and it charges on its own.
What's the difference between a ceiling lift and a floor lift?
A floor (portable) lift sits on the ground and must be manually wheeled into position. Ceiling lifts mount overhead and take up zero floor space. Ceiling lifts are easier to operate, require less physical effort from caregivers, don't need to be stored when not in use, and can cover room-to-room paths that floor lifts can't navigate. For permanent home installations, ceiling lifts are almost always the better solution.

Wheelchair Ramps

How long does a wheelchair ramp need to be?
ADA guidelines recommend a 1:12 slope ratio — 1 foot of ramp length for every inch of vertical rise. For example, a home with a 24-inch entry step needs approximately 24 feet of ramp run. This can be configured in a straight run, L-shape, U-shape, or switchback to fit your available space. We design the optimal layout during your site assessment.
Do modular ramps require a building permit?
Requirements vary by municipality. In many Oregon jurisdictions, modular ramp systems that don't permanently attach to the structure are exempt from building permits. However, some cities require permits for any exterior modification. We research the specific requirements for your location and handle the permit process if one is needed.
Can a modular ramp be relocated?
Yes. EZ Access PATHWAY 3G ramps are bolt-together modular systems that can be fully disassembled and reassembled at a new location. This makes them ideal for renters, temporary recovery situations, or if you move to a different home. The components are reusable with minimal waste.
Will an aluminum ramp get slippery in the rain?
No. EZ Access ramps feature either extruded tread surfaces with raised ribs or expanded metal with diamond-shaped openings — both engineered specifically for wet-weather traction. Portland's rainy climate was a major factor in why we chose EZ Access as our ramp partner. The aluminum surface also won't rust, rot, or decay regardless of weather exposure.
What handrail options are available?
EZ Access offers three handrail styles: two-line rails (the most popular — with an upper rail and a lower gripping surface), vertical pickets (for a more residential look), and horizontal pickets (for safety with a modern aesthetic). All meet ADA height and grip requirements.
What's the weight capacity of a PATHWAY 3G ramp?
The PATHWAY 3G system has an 850 lb weight capacity per section, which accommodates powered wheelchairs, scooters, and standard foot traffic with a wide safety margin. The ramps are 36 inches wide (clear passage width), meeting ADA minimum width requirements.

Stairlifts

How wide does my staircase need to be for a stairlift?
Most Harmar stairlifts require a minimum staircase width of about 26–28 inches. When folded, the unit protrudes only 10.5 inches from the wall, leaving plenty of room for other household members to walk the stairs normally.
What happens during a power outage?
Harmar stairlifts run on battery power that charges continuously from a standard household outlet. The backup battery provides up to 60 full trips during a power outage — so you'll never be stranded on the staircase. The system automatically resumes charging when power is restored.
Is the stairlift attached to the wall or the stairs?
The rail mounts directly to the stair treads — not the wall. This means no wall damage, no structural concerns, and a cleaner installation. Rail brackets only need to be attached to select stairs (not every step), and the system can be removed later with minimal repair needed.
Can a stairlift handle curved stairs?
NW Homecare Medical specializes in Harmar straight-run stairlifts, which are designed for standard straight staircases. If your staircase has curves, intermediate landings, or unusual configurations, contact us — we can discuss whether a solution exists or refer you to a curved stairlift specialist.
Will a stairlift block the stairway for other people?
No. When not in use, the seat, armrests, and footrest all fold up, and the unit sits just 10.5 inches from the wall. Optional folding rail sections are available if the bottom of the rail extends past a doorway or hallway — keeping the path completely clear.
How much maintenance does a stairlift need?
Very little. Harmar stairlifts use a grease-free helical worm gear drive system that requires minimal maintenance and won't leave marks on your staircase. Periodically wiping down the rail and checking that the battery is charging is typically all that's needed. We cover maintenance guidance during your post-installation training.

Safety & Planning Ahead

When is the right time to make aging in place modifications?
The best time is before you need them. Experts recommend acting proactively — when stairs start feeling more tiring, when you grip the railing tighter than before, when you start avoiding certain rooms, or when a near-fall makes you think twice. Getting ahead of mobility changes gives you time to research options, plan a budget, and adapt comfortably — rather than making rushed decisions after an injury or hospital discharge.
What are the most common fall hazards in a home?
The most dangerous areas are:
  • Bathroom: Slippery tub/shower surfaces, high tub walls to step over, lack of grab bars
  • Stairs: Steep or narrow staircases, worn carpet, weak or missing railings
  • Entryways: Steps at exterior doors, uneven thresholds, no ramp access
  • Hallways: Poor lighting, narrow passages, loose rugs
Our whole-home assessment identifies all of these hazards and prioritizes modifications by impact and urgency.
Can modifications help someone recovering from surgery or injury?
Absolutely. Many of our clients need modifications for temporary recovery — a hip replacement, knee surgery, stroke recovery, or accident rehabilitation. Products like modular ramps and stairlifts can be installed quickly, used during recovery, and removed or relocated afterward. A ceiling lift can make the difference between recovering at home versus a rehab facility.
What if I'm planning for a family member with dementia or Alzheimer's?
We work with families navigating progressive conditions. Modifications often focus on fall prevention (grab bars, barrier-free showers, removing trip hazards), safe transfers (ceiling lifts for when mobility declines), and maintaining dignity and independence for as long as possible. We can design a phased plan that addresses immediate needs while anticipating future changes.
How do multigenerational households benefit from aging in place modifications?
Multigenerational living is increasingly common, and accessibility modifications benefit everyone — not just the senior members. A barrier-free shower is safer for all ages. A ramp helps with strollers, bikes, and deliveries. A stairlift can serve an aging parent today and a recovering teenager tomorrow. We design modifications that integrate naturally into the home for the whole family.
